> “I am not sure whether there can be a way of really understanding the miracle of thinking.” — Einstein, quoted in Wertheimer (1959, p. 227)

People with OCD often report the belief that thinking an intrusive thought makes the content of the thought more likely to occur. Rachman and Shafran (1999) give this description of a 32-year-old patient suffering from such thought–action fusion:

> “Each night he dreaded going to bed because he feared that he might die in his sleep; he rated the probability of such an untimely death as 10 to 20%. However, he rated the probability of the therapist or relatives or friends dying in the same fashion as less than one in a million. When asked to explain this bias, why he in particular was at such a high risk, he replied that he was more likely to die in this way precisely because he experienced the thought that he might do so” (Rachman & Shafran, 1999, p. 80).

If you strongly expect your thoughts to be causally connected to external events, and the thoughts that come to you are involuntary and horrible, you’re in a bad place. Interestingly, most people have some superstitious expectations of this kind. If you want to test this, try the following: take a piece of paper and a pen and write down “I sincerely hope that &lt;insert loved one’s name&gt; horribly suffocates in their sleep tomorrow morning at 3 a.m.” Now close your eyes and think about the situation for a short moment.

In certain types of therapy (metacognitive approaches in particular), thought–action fusion is tackled by conveying that intrusive thoughts are transient and haphazard phenomena. Just let them pass through. A thought like “I am going to die in my sleep” does not usually make it more likely that you will.

On the other hand, thoughts are of course related to other events. You may look in the mirror today and think “gosh, I could really use a haircut” and find yourself, two days from now, seeking out a person with large scissors to chop off your hair. Thoughts co-arise with all sorts of other phenomena in all sorts of suspicious ways.

And a lifetime of experiencing contingencies between thoughts and events creates big variations in when and how strongly we expect our thoughts to come true. In the course of this process, we also develop broader and very individual relationships with our mental content. We form an implicit stance with which we approach and relate to thoughts.

This implicit stance is not some kind of meta-belief. It’s not about confidence in the veracity of statements encoded in thoughts, or confidence in the confidence, or some further removed cognitive concept. It’s more like how your consciousness meets another person, but the other person is a thought.

Metacognitive therapies do not only try to convey that intrusive thoughts are transient and haphazard. They also try to address this implicit stance. Take again the thought “I will die in my sleep tonight.” Before metacognitive therapy, a patient may think the thought and have the expectation that thinking it makes dying in their sleep more likely. They fret, try to suppress the thought, avoid going to sleep and so on. After therapy, they may still have the same thoughts (both the intrusive thought and the sense that thinking it makes it more likely), but the thoughts are no longer so intimately related to them. The relationship has changed.

Taking this perspective, you and thought can be viewed as relational entities that interact with each other. One striking property that a thought can then have: agency – the capacity to reorganize your world. Some thoughts barely touch the rest of your life, whereas others influence everything around you.

Here’s an illustration of this. Like everyone, I have thoughts about artificial intelligence. I find it a fascinating and important topic. I also think there is considerable risk of really bad outcomes. If pressed, I could produce a number and a confidence interval and whatever else to quantify this risk. But when these thoughts wiggle, the effect on the rest of my world is, for some reason, small. Consequently, I feel that I tend to approach the topic with a bit of a detached nonchalance.

I noticed this in a conversation with a good friend. They are also worried. But their thoughts intertwine much more closely with their reality than mine do. When his thoughts wiggle, all sorts of aspects of his world are touched and start wiggling, too. The different relationship is very tangible: my friend is more viscerally alive in relation to these thoughts. More engaged, more concerned, more trusting, more serious.

Almost as if his thoughts were important people in his life, acting within his world. Mine are more like distant cousins. Familiar, interesting to talk to at that wedding. Then you might not see them for years. My friend’s thoughts about AI risks have agentic qualities in his world. My distant cousins, by contrast, are not meaningful agents in mine.

Thoughts can have extremely deep agentic qualities. Einstein, for instance, wondered from a young age what things would look like if he were sitting on a beam of light. “The germ of the special relativity theory is already contained” in this Gedankenexperiment, he later said (Einstein, 1949, p. 53). For Einstein, these thoughts were clearly extremely agentic. They had profound repercussions within his reality[^1], and he related to them in an extraordinarily trusting, diligent, even compulsive manner over many years.

[^1]: And to our great luck, his reality was very closely related to physical reality.

Coming back to OCD: for our patient, the thought “I will die in my sleep tonight” may be very agentic. It may not make dying in their sleep more likely, but it certainly does rearrange the reality of the thinker. It causes fear, anxiety, attempts to neutralize and counteract and so on. Treating such an uncontrollable intrusive thought as if it were an agent able to wreak havoc in your life is bad. Learning to dislodge it from its place in your world, as is the invitation of metacognitive therapies, is good.

But this is not necessarily a good approach to other thoughts. Consider what would have happened had Einstein met his compulsive thinking about thought experiments with metacognitive training: “Ah, transient mental event, let’s just let it pass.”
